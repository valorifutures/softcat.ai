When a major consultancy quietly tells its own staff that their billing model is finished, that is not a warning. That is a confession. The billable hour was never a measure of value. It was a measure of time spent looking like value. AI agents do not play that game.

## The Model Was Always the Problem

Charging clients by the hour made sense when information was scarce and expertise was genuinely hard to access. Neither of those things is true anymore. What AI has done is make the gap between effort and output visible in a way that is very uncomfortable for anyone who built a career on the gap being invisible.

Agents can draft, analyse, summarise, and synthesise in minutes. That does not mean consultants are useless. It means the ones who were coasting on process and padding are about to find out.

## What Survives the Transition

The consultants worth keeping are the ones who were never really selling hours anyway. They were selling judgement, relationships, and the ability to say something uncomfortable in a room full of people who do not want to hear it. No agent does that well yet.

The real shift is structural. Firms built around headcount and utilisation rates are going to have to rebuild around outcomes and accountability. That is genuinely harder. Selling a hundred hours of work is easy. Putting a price on a decision that changes a business is a different skill entirely.

The firms that figure out outcome-based pricing first will own this decade. The ones still debating whether AI is a threat or an opportunity will find out the answer the slow way.
