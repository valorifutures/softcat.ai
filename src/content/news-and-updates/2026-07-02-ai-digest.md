A busy week for AI infrastructure drama, corporate overreach, and a few stories that sound minor but really aren't.

## Anthropic hid code in Claude Code that flagged Chinese users

This is the most important story of the week. [Hidden monitoring logic inside Claude Code](https://the-decoder.com/hidden-code-in-claude-code-secretly-flagged-chinese-users/) was silently tagging users it identified as Chinese, without disclosure. Anthropic is removing it after the backlash, but the damage to trust is real. If you're building on top of Anthropic's tools, this is worth thinking hard about.

## Claude Sonnet 5 is quietly much more expensive than it looks

The per-token price hasn't changed, but [Claude Sonnet 5 uses roughly 40% more tokens per task than its predecessor](https://the-decoder.com/claude-sonnet-5-continues-anthropics-pattern-of-hiding-price-increases-behind-unchanged-token-rates/), nearly doubling real-world costs. The model is genuinely good and beats Opus 4.8 on some agent tasks, but this pattern of hiding price increases behind token inflation is getting old. Worth factoring into any cost modelling you're doing.

## Meta is building a cloud business to sell spare AI compute

[Meta is planning to sell excess compute capacity to outside customers](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/01/meta-like-spacex-looks-to-turn-excess-ai-compute-into-cash/), putting it in direct competition with AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure. With $145 billion in AI investment planned this year, it makes sense to sweat those assets. Whether enterprise buyers will trust Meta with their workloads is a different question.

## Cloudflare draws a line on AI training crawlers

[Cloudflare is giving AI companies until 15 September](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/01/cloudflares-new-policy-pushes-ai-companies-to-pay-for-publishers-content/) to separate search crawlers from training crawlers, or get blocked across publisher sites by default. This is a meaningful shift in how content access gets negotiated at scale. The real test is whether publishers actually enforce it.

## Venice AI hits unicorn status with a privacy-first model

[Venice AI raised a $65M Series A and is already profitable](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/01/venice-ai-becomes-a-unicorn-with-65m-series-a-as-its-privacy-first-ai-platform-takes-off/), with over $70M in annualised revenue. Privacy-first AI is clearly finding a real market, not just a niche one. Good signal that users actually care about where their data goes.
